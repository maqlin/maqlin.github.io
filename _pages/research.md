## Research interests

- Galaxy Morphology

Galaxies exhibit diverse morphologies ranging from bulge-dominated to disk-dominated systems—a classification known as the Hubble sequence. Galaxy morphology has long been recognized as one of the most fundamental properties of galaxies. With the help of JWST and ALMA, massive rotating disks have been detected at high redshifts, prompting a critical question: how does galaxy morphology evolve, and what physical processes drive these changes?

Using the COLIBRE simulations, we identify a three-stage evolutionary path for massive galaxies, transitioning from dispersion-dominated to disk-dominated, and eventually back to dispersion-dominated states. Furthermore, we decompose the specific physical processes occurring at each stage, particularly during the formation and subsequent transformation of the disk (Ma et.al in prep).

Using the FIRE-2 simulations, we focus on Milky Way-like galaxies. We find that the timing of disk formation is intrinsically linked to the transition of the dark matter halo from a rapid accretion phase to a slow accretion phase. Once the halo enters the slow accretion regime, the gravitational potential becomes sufficiently deep to allow gas to settle, and be further consumed to form an inner bulge followed by the gradual development of the stellar disk [Ma et.al 2025b](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2509.16138).

- Ultra diffuse galaxies

Ultra-diffuse galaxies (UDGs) serve as extreme laboratories for testing galaxy formation and the nature of dark matter due to their low stellar densities and extended structures. 
Recent SDSS observations have uncovered a puzzle: isolated, blue UDGs exhibit unexpectedly strong large-scale clustering, comparable to massive galaxy groups—a signal that standard LCDM simulations like IllustrisTNG and traditional subhalo models fail to replicate.
By analyzing the TNG300-1-Dark simulation, we demonstrate that this discrepancy is caused by the definition of halo spin. While spin based solely on bound particles fails to explain the data, a definition incorporating the entire local environment (including unbound material) produces a strong spin bias. 
By linking galaxy surface brightness to this environmental angular momentum in mock catalogs, we successfully reproduce the observed UDG clustering within the LCDM framework. Our results suggest that UDG clustering is a signature of galaxy assembly bias rather than evidence for alternative dark matter physics [Ma et al. 2025c](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.17742).

- Dark matter halos

The Large-Scale Structure (LSS) governs the formation and evolution of dark matter halos and their resident galaxies.

On one hand, inspired by the observed overabundance of satellites along and between the Milky Way and M31 in the Local Group, we utilized the SDSS survey to extend this study to a much broader mass range. We systematically confirmed the existence of this lopsidedness in satellite distributions [Guo, Ma & Li 2025](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2502.14666). By constructing mock catalogs, we found that this phenomenon is primarily driven by the fact that galaxy pairs tend to align with cosmic filaments [Ma et al. 2025a](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.14668). This geometric alignment leads to an excess of satellites along the pair axis, suggesting that the observed lopsidedness is a manifestation of the underlying cosmic web.

On the other hand, we are currently investigating how the anisotropy of the large-scale environment exerts torques on dark matter halos, generating strong tidal flows. These flows shape the secondary properties of the halos, ultimately giving rise to halo assembly bias (or secondary bias) (Ma et al. in prep).
